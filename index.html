Reflections

Paper Prototype

I chose the phrase ‘the net is anarchic and subversive’ and made a concrete poem of it. As seen, the visual arrangement of the words convey meaning. The words cascade and shift to create a chaotic and unpredictable pattern, reflecting the chaotic nature of the internet. The phrase "the net is anarchic and subversive" also suggests that the internet is a space of disorder and disruption. My paper prototype is meant to represent these ideas visually and interactively. The prototype includes a piece of paper with a majority of the phrase written down, along with strips of paper weaved into it to create moving elements that ‘reveal’ the hidden words when interacted with. This is to simulate the unpredictable and fluid nature of the internet. There is a mouse that the user can use to move a cursor to interact with the blue and green buttons to ‘reveal’ the hidden words.

Reflecting on my experience with paper prototypes, I’ve realised just how valuable they are in the design process. Working with something as simple as paper allowed me to strip back all the bells and whistles and really focus on the core functionality of my design. It forced me to think about the user’s journey in a way that digital tools sometimes overshadow with their complexity. I was able to quickly sketch ideas, test them, and then easily make changes without worrying about the technical side of things. This freedom to explore and fail early on has been a game changer.

One of the biggest takeaways for me was how paper prototypes encouraged rapid iteration. In a matter of minutes, I could completely rework the layout or flow based on feedback or a fresh idea. The simplicity of the medium made it easy to tweak things, which really opened up more opportunities for creativity. It taught me not to get too attached to any one design early in the process, which in turn made me more open to new ideas and directions.

User testing with paper prototypes has been especially eye-opening. Seeing how people interact with a design in its most basic form showed me where potential problems might arise, even before moving to a digital version. It’s one thing to imagine how users might behave, but quite another to watch them fumble or navigate naturally through the prototype. I learned how crucial it is to gather user feedback early that I might have overlooked if I went straight to a high-fidelity design.

Overall, working with paper prototypes has reinforced the importance of focusing on functionality first. It’s so easy to get caught up in the aesthetics of a project, but these prototypes reminded me that if the core experience doesn’t work, no amount of visual polish can save it. This approach has made me a more thoughtful, user-centred designer, and I’ll carry these lessons forward.

Major Project

My webpage is an interactive visual experience that is further developed based on the quote, "The net is anarchic and subversive", which was the text for my paper prototype. The design dynamically engages users by presenting a blend of chaotic and orderly elements, reflecting the unpredictable and free-flowing nature of the internet. Users are invited to explore the webpage by interacting with various elements such as clicking, hovering, and moving the mouse which causes letters to move or fly out, offering a sense of motion and transformation.

The visual focus shifts between two distinct background images featuring iconic characters (Jhin and Yasuo) from the game League of Legends, which can be toggled by user action. Additionally, certain keywords in the displayed quote, "anarchic" and "subversive," trigger deeper insights by revealing contextual definitions. The typography is randomised, with text sizes, fonts, and colours varying across letters, adding a sense of creative chaos in a way.

In building this webpage, my goal was to reflect on the dual nature of the internet as both a space of freedom and disruption. The quote, "The net is anarchic and subversive," encapsulates the tension between the endless opportunities of online spaces and the challenge of order in a seemingly chaotic environment. Through the interactive elements, I wanted users to experience a sense of this unpredictability such as letters scattering upon hovering and then realigning, while important words remain grounded, representing the core ideas in the chaos.

This project allowed me to explore the use of arrays, conditional statements, and interactive elements in JavaScript to manipulate visuals in real-time. One of the most rewarding aspects was the implementation of mouse events to trigger animations, such as letters flying out when interacted with. I feel this gave the webpage a fun but controlled interaction, reacting to user behaviour and enhancing the immersive experience.

The choice of background images and the thematic connection to characters like Jhin and Yasuo from League of Legends offered a nod to chaos and order. Jhin being methodical and Yasuo representing freedom, symbolising the complex dynamic I wanted to capture. This project also deepened my understanding of balancing aesthetics with functionality, ensuring the randomness of font changes did not interfere with readability but rather enhanced the chaotic essence of the page.

In conclusion, the webpage's structure serves as both an artistic and coding exercise, pushing me to think about how visuals, interactivity, and user engagement can all merge to convey a thematic message.

Class Activities

Studio Arcade

During week 1 of the studio class, my group and I created a paper arcade game prototype, inspired by the classic Pac-Man arcade game. The project involved cutting out shapes to form the maze, movable characters, and the iconic elements of Pac-Man and the ghosts. The interactivity was achieved through sliding mechanisms that allowed the characters to move within the maze, giving a sense of gameplay and movement without the need for digital components. This tangible, hands-on creation captured the essence of the arcade game in a simplified form, encouraging players to engage physically with the elements. In our studio, we gathered around the prototype, observing how people interacted with it, and it was fascinating to see how such a simple model could spark discussions on movement, user experience, and design.

This experience reinforced the value of paper prototypes in the design process. By constructing a low-fidelity version of a concept, we were able to quickly test out ideas, understand spatial relationships, and gather feedback from others. Paper prototypes, like this arcade game, serve as a critical tool in the early stages of design. They allow for iterative adjustments without the need for costly or time-consuming digital development. By working with physical materials, you can better understand scale, user interaction, and how elements might function in real life. This form of prototyping also facilitates collaboration, as others can easily visualise and interact with the concept, offering insights and feedback that are vital to refining the design. It’s a playful and practical way to explore the possibilities before committing to more complex mediums.

Interpret Drawings As Machines

During week 2 of the studio class, we engaged with the idea of interpreting drawings as machine instructions, translating visual elements into a set of precise commands that could be understood by a machine. The process began with a simple sketch of a face made up of geometric shapes, triangles, rectangles, and circles, along with annotations specifying the mathematical instructions necessary for their creation. The notations included details such as the x and y coordinates, the sizes of the shapes, and reflections along the axis. This approach pushed me to think of drawings not only as visual representations but also as codes that can be executed step by step to create a digital equivalent.

This exercise highlighted how design and code often intersect. While design typically relies on intuition, creativity, and a sense of form, coding demands precision, logic, and structure. In this case, the drawing had to be broken down into a series of logical steps, much like how a computer program operates. The process involved analysing each shape in terms of its dimensions, its relation to other shapes, and its positioning in a Cartesian coordinate system. By doing so, I could essentially “instruct” a machine to replicate the drawing with exact accuracy.

What I found particularly interesting was how this exercise made me more aware of the importance of clarity and precision in both design and code. When creating visual work by hand, it’s easy to overlook the fine details, but when those details must be communicated as machine-readable instructions, every line and position matters. This taught me to view design through a more technical lens, where even the smallest change can affect the outcome.

Concrete Poetry

During week 3 of my studio class, we experimented with concrete poetry. I chose to focus on the phrase "An error has been detected in your consciousness. All source-codes are corrupt. Continue?" This text represents a blend of digital and human elements, using the language of programming and error detection to metaphorically comment on the nature of human awareness and thought. The layout of the text reinforces the sensation of an error or disruption, with the repetition of "consciousness" and "corrupt" driving home the theme of malfunction. In this way, the work invites reflection on our increasingly intertwined relationships with technology and its impact on our perception of self.

The concept of concrete poetry ties closely to design in its ability to use form to communicate meaning. The way the words are arranged on the page is just as important as the words themselves, merging visual design with literary content. This exercise pushed me to think about how text can transcend its linguistic function and become a visual element in design. In this case, the repetition of phrases and the compact, uniform style reflect both the rigidity and fragility of digital systems and how those same ideas might apply to human cognition. This merging of code and consciousness creates a poetic critique of how dependent we have become on technology, and how that dependency might be affecting our mental and emotional frameworks.

Instructional Drawings and Crazy 8

During week 4 of my studio class, we did instructional-based drawings as a method to simulate how we input instructions when coding. The experience was eye-opening, as it involved translating visual ideas into clear, step-by-step directives that could be interpreted both by a machine and a human. This exercise challenged me to think about clarity and precision—qualities that are essential in both design and coding. In the same way that machines require exact commands, instructional drawings necessitate well-thought-out details that leave little room for ambiguity. I found myself questioning not just how I draw, but how I communicate a process visually, ensuring that someone else could replicate the steps without confusion.

Simultaneously, we worked on crazy 8 ideas, which encouraged rapid brainstorming and ideation. In just eight minutes, we had to come up with eight different ideas, pushing the limits of creativity and quick thinking. The constraint of time forced me to let go of perfectionism and embrace spontaneity, a valuable reminder that not every idea needs to be fully formed in the early stages. This fast-paced ideation process is akin to how coding sometimes involves trial and error, where you test out ideas, discard the ones that don’t work, and refine the ones that do.
